## What Happened

Hayao Miyazaki, the creative genius behind beloved films like "My Neighbor Totoro" and "Spirited Away," designed what may be the world's most elaborate cuckoo clock. The Giant Ghibli Clock, officially called the "Ni-Tele Really Big Clock," measures 12 meters tall by 18 meters wide and weighs an astounding 28 tons. Built by master sculptor Kunio Shachimaru, the clock features over 30 separate moving mechanical components, all hand-carved from copper and steel.

The clock performs three-minute mechanical shows four times daily on weekdays and five times on weekends. During these performances, blacksmiths hammer at anvils, teapots release steam, cannons fire, and various Ghibli-inspired figures dance and move in perfect synchronization. The entire spectacle feels like watching one of Miyazaki's films come to life in three dimensions.

## Why It Matters

This clock represents something increasingly rare in our digital age: public art that requires no smartphone, no app, no payment—just the simple act of looking up. In Tokyo's ultra-modern business district, where glass towers and neon dominate the landscape, Miyazaki's creation offers a moment of analog wonder that connects passersby to the handcrafted artistry of a bygone era.

The project also demonstrates Miyazaki's commitment to bringing joy beyond the cinema screen. Rather than simply licensing his characters for commercial products, he spent six years personally designing every gear, lever, and figure to create something genuinely magical for the public. The clock serves as a physical manifestation of Studio Ghibli's philosophy: that wonder and imagination can transform ordinary moments into something extraordinary.

## Background

Mechanical clocks have long served as more than mere timekeepers—they're often elaborate artistic statements that reflect their creators' values and their era's technological capabilities. From Prague's famous 14th-century astronomical clock to Paris's "Defender of Time" (where a man battles mythical creatures), these timepieces blend function with storytelling.

Miyazaki's clock continues this tradition while adding his signature touch: the celebration of craftsmanship, nature, and the magic found in everyday objects. The animator, known for his meticulous attention to detail and preference for hand-drawn animation over digital techniques, approached the clock project with the same philosophical principles that guide his films.

The collaboration with Kunio Shachimaru, a master sculptor experienced in large-scale mechanical art, was essential to translating Miyazaki's two-dimensional vision into a three-dimensional reality. The six-year construction timeline reflects the painstaking attention to detail both artists demanded—every moving part had to work flawlessly while maintaining the aesthetic integrity of Miyazaki's design.

## What's Next

The Giant Ghibli Clock has become a beloved landmark, drawing both tourists and locals who time their commutes to catch the daily performances. Its success has inspired discussions about incorporating more public art into urban environments, particularly art that engages viewers without requiring digital interaction.

For Studio Ghibli fans, the clock serves as a pilgrimage site and a tangible connection to Miyazaki's creative world. As the director continues work on what may be his final film, this mechanical masterpiece stands as a permanent testament to his belief that art should bring wonder to everyday life.

The clock also represents a broader movement toward "slow technology"—mechanical devices that operate on human timescales rather than digital speed. In an era of instant everything, watching gears turn and figures move at their own deliberate pace offers a form of meditation that many find increasingly valuable.
